# Mentor notes — Technical Interviews

HackYourFuture Denmark — Talk + Q&A (~90–120 min)

Students: 9 months of education, frontend + backend tracks (separate), essentially no professional experience. All junior candidates.

---

## Core frame for this session

Interviews aren't standardized tests — they reflect the person running them. The three personas give students a mental model to *read* the room and adapt, rather than assume one universal process.

The anti-LeetCode instinct is real, but the more useful message is: *you will meet all three types, and each one tells you something different about how to show up*.

---

## The three personas

### The Professor
- Academic/structured mindset. Often CS-trained or big-tech background.
- Tests fundamentals, reasoning under constraints.
- Least likely for a first HYF job — but students should know it exists.
- Key coaching: don't freeze. Narrate. A partial answer explained out loud beats silence and a correct one.

### The Teammate
- Collaborative, pair-programming style. Your style.
- Tests: will I enjoy working with this person?
- **Most learnable type for juniors** — communication and curiosity matter more than depth.
- Key coaching: ask questions, stay curious, take feedback without getting defensive.

### The Builder
- Take-home or async. Trusts output over live performance.
- Tests: code quality, judgment, do they understand *why* something was wrong.
- **Most prep-friendly for HYF students** — their projects are direct practice.
- Key coaching: treat it like real work, leave the code better, add a short explanation.

## The live demo (slides 13–15)

**The scenario:** filtering a list of items by a search term.

Each persona gets a different framing of the same underlying problem:

**The Professor** — "Write a function that takes an array and a search term, returns matching items. Don't use `.filter()` — implement the loop yourself."
- Narrate: start with an empty array, explain the loop, think out loud even when stuck.

**The Teammate** — "I've got a search bar that's not filtering correctly. Let's look at it together."
- Demo: pair debugging. Ask questions. Say what you see. Propose a change and explain why.
- Things to model: "Can I ask — what should it do exactly?", "I think I see the issue, can I try something?"

**The Builder** — "Here's a broken search feature. Fix it, add a comment explaining what was wrong, send your branch by Friday."
- Demo: find the bug, write a minimal fix, leave a clear one-line comment.
- Key point: the goal isn't showing off — it's showing judgment.

## What they all care about (slide 9)

Evaluation order to walk through:
1. How they think — reason out loud, get themselves unstuck
2. Technical knowledge — not memorisation, applying what they know
3. Communication — explain what they're doing and why

**The myths to bust:**
- "I must solve it perfectly" — false
- "Asking for a hint means I failed" — false
- "Fast coding = strong engineer" — false

---

## The junior reframe (slide 11)

> "A junior who can explain what they built, why they made those choices, and what they'd do differently is already ahead of most candidates at their level."

This is the core message. You're competing on *clarity*, not depth.

---

## The honest story (slide 17)

Tell the story of a candidate you believed in who didn't get through because of interview performance, not ability. The point: interviewing is a separate skill. It's worth preparing for seriously, not because the process is fair — but because it matters.

---

## One thing this week (slide 19)

Give them exactly one of these — not all three:
- **Option A:** Explain a project out loud to someone non-technical. Time yourself. Notice where you get stuck.
- **Option B:** Record one answer and listen back.
- **Option C:** 10-minute mock interview with a classmate, swap roles.

---

## The honest caveat (slide 20)

This session reflects your experience. Some companies run very different processes (FAANG-style grinding). What travels everywhere: thinking clearly and communicating well.

---

## Things to watch for in Q&A

- "What if I really don't know the answer?" → narrate what you do know, name the gap honestly, show how you'd approach learning it
- "How do I prepare for The Professor if I haven't practiced algorithms?" → focus on a few common patterns (loops, string matching, basic data structures) and practice thinking out loud more than grinding problems
- "What if they ask about something I've never heard of?" → "I haven't worked with that, but here's how I'd approach learning it" is a genuinely strong answer
