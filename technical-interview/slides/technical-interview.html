Part 01 · Why interviews vary

An interview is not a standardized test

What people assume
An objective test. A right way to answer. A score that says yes or no.
What it actually is
One engineer’s best attempt to answer: “Can this person do the job?” Filtered through their background, their company’s culture, and sometimes guidelines handed to them.
That’s why every interview feels different. Because they are.
Part 01 · Why interviews vary

Some types of interviewer you’ll meet

The Professor
Tests your technical foundations. Wants to see how you think under structure.
The Teammate
Wants to know if they’d enjoy working with you. Cares how you think out loud.
The Builder
Doesn’t trust live performance. Wants to see what you actually produce.
These are patterns, not labels. Use them to read the room.
Part 02 · Interviewer types

The Professor

Who they are
Came up through a CS program or big tech. Believes algorithms show how engineers think. Often uses a structured rubric.
What it looks like
Timed coding problems. Data structure questions. “Write a function that…” without using built-in methods.
For you
Hardest type without years of prep and least likely for a first role. Focus on narrating what you know, even without the answer.
Part 02 · Interviewer types

The Teammate

Who they are
Cares about how you’ll work every day. Pairs with their team constantly. Interviews the way they’d spend a Tuesday at the office.
What it looks like
Pair programming on a real problem. Walkthrough of your past project. “Let’s look at this together.”
For you
This is where juniors can outshine. Being clear and easy to work with matters more than knowing every answer.
Part 02 · Interviewer types

The Builder

Who they are
Doesn’t trust live performance. Wants to see what you produce when left alone with a real task, no audience.
What it looks like
A take-home project. “Debug this codebase.” “Add this feature.” Asynchronous, with a deadline.
For you
Your HYF projects are direct prep. Having a portfolio and being able to explain your decisions is a real advantage here.
Part 02 · Interviewer types

What they sound like

The Professor
“You have 40 minutes. Write a function that finds all duplicates in an array. Do not use built-in dedup methods.”
The Teammate
“Walk me through the last project you built. What decision are you most proud of? What would you change?”
The Builder
“Here’s a small app with three failing tests. Get them passing. Send us your branch by Friday.”
Part 02 · Interviewer types

These are patterns, not categories

Real interviewers are a mix. The same company might give you a take-home and a pair programming session on the same day. One person might start like The Professor and shift into The Teammate halfway through.
Use the types to read the room, not to predict it. The moment you notice how someone is interviewing, you can adjust.
Part 03 · What they all care about

Underneath it all, they want to understand:

1
How you think — do you reason out loud? Can you get yourself unstuck?
2
What you know technically — not memorization, but applying what you know
3
How you communicate — can you explain what you’re doing and why?
These signals show up in every type of interview, in every company.
Part 03 · What they all care about

Things you can stop worrying about

“I must solve it perfectly”
A perfect solution with no communication is worth less than a messy one explained clearly.
“Asking for a hint means I failed”
Using hints well is itself a positive signal. Ask good questions. Build on what you’re given.
“Coding fast = being a strong engineer”
Speed is not what any interviewer is measuring. Clarity is.
Part 03 · What they all care about

You are not competing on depth

A junior who can explain what they built, why they made those choices, and what they’d do differently is already ahead of most candidates at their level.
You’re competing on clarity. Not on years of experience. Not on knowing every API.
Part 04 · Live demo

The same scenario. Three interviews.

One task: filtering a list. Three different interviewers. Three completely different experiences.
The Professor
Write the algorithm yourself
The Teammate
Let’s figure it out together
The Builder
Here’s a broken feature. Fix it.
Part 04 · The Professor

The Professor’s problem

// items: array, term: string // Return items matching term by name. // Do NOT use .filter(). // Write the loop yourself.
function search(items, term) {
  // your solution here
}
What they watch for
Do you talk while you think? What do you do when you’re not sure?
Strong junior answer
“I’d start with an empty array and loop through. I’m going to think out loud as I go…” Then keep going, even if it’s messy.
Part 04 · The Teammate

The Teammate’s problem

The setup
“I’ve started building a search bar but it’s not filtering correctly. Let’s look at it together. What do you see?”
What they watch for
Do you ask questions? Share your thinking? How do you respond when they suggest something?
Strong junior answer
“Can I ask what it’s supposed to do? I’m looking at this line. It might be comparing the wrong thing. Want me to try changing it?”
The goal
Show you can work with another person, not just next to them.
Part 04 · The Builder

The Builder’s problem

The task (by email)
“The search bar isn’t filtering. Fix it. Add a short comment explaining what was wrong. Send your branch by Friday.”
What they watch for
Is the fix readable? Did they understand why it was broken, not just make it work?
Strong junior answer
Clean, minimal fix. One-line comment: “Was comparing full objects instead of names. Changed to item.name.”
The goal
Treat it like real work. Don’t over-engineer. Leave it better than you found it.
Part 05 · Interview formats

Formats you’ll encounter

Whiteboard — live coding on screen or a physical board, often timed
Take-home — async project you complete alone, judged on quality and judgment
Pair programming — you and the interviewer solve something together in real time
Technical phone screen — quick questions over a call, usually a first filter
System design — talk through how you’d architect something (rare at junior level)
Part 06 · The human side

Good interviewers are on your side

Interviewers know this is a nerve-inducing situation. They’ve been on that side of the table too.
A good interviewer’s job is to help you show your best, not to catch you out.
What helps
→ Say when you’re not sure. It’s allowed.
→ Pause and think. Silence isn’t failure.
→ Ask questions. Clarifying what’s expected is a good sign.
→ Think with the interviewer, not for them.
Part 06 · The human side

What we’re listening for
Nerves are normal. Did the interviewer help or make it worse? What made the difference?

Interviewing is a skill. Separate from your ability to do the job.
Part 06 · The human side

Things you can say out loud

“I’m not sure yet. Let me think through what I do know.”
“Can I ask a quick question before I start?”
“I’d approach it this way. But I can see another option. Should I keep going or discuss the tradeoffs?”
“I haven’t used that before, but here’s how I’d approach learning it.”
